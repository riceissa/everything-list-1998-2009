> This is because if consciousness is a computational process then it is
> independant of the (physical or ... virtual) implementation. If I
> perfom the computation on an abacus or within my head or with stones
> on the ground... it is the same (from the computation pov).
>
> And that's my problem with physicalism. How can it account for the
> independance of implementation if computations are not real ?

Just because a computation is independent of any particular
realization it does not follow that it need not be realized at all.

It seems to me that the realization of a computation is always
relative to a context. So if computation implements consciousness and
it is realized by the thermal motions of molecules in a rock, that
consciousness is relative to some "rock world". I know Stathis
questions with this because he thinks of dreams and imagination and
other conscious thoughts that are "in our heads" and don't involve
interaction with the external world. But I think they necessarily
refer to and gain their meaning by reference to such interaction.
